About the Whole Spine Collection

Explore our detailed collection of individually segmented human vertebrae, presented as high-resolution 3D models for ethical research and educational purposes. Each vertebra has been meticulously scanned and digitized using advanced 3D scanning technologies, ensuring precise anatomical accuracy while preserving the integrity of the original remains.

This collection allows for an in-depth examination of vertebral morphology, aiding in research on biomechanics, pathology, and skeletal identification. By providing access to these digital models, we support ethical anthropological study while minimizing the physical handling of human remains, aligning with principles of respectful research and repatriation.

This spine (#15) was rearticulated by Ayumi Escalante under the guidance of Dr. Rashidi in the UCLA Anthropology Lab.

Vertebral Anatomy

Each vertebra consists of several key anatomical features:

  • Body
  • Arch
  • Processes
  • Foramina

Vertebral Body

The vertebral body is the weight-bearing portion of the vertebra. It is the large, cylindrical or kidney-shaped anterior portion that increases in size as you move down the spine, reflecting the increasing weight-bearing requirements. The superior and inferior surfaces articulate with adjacent vertebrae, separated by intervertebral discs in life.

Key features of the vertebral body include:

  • Superior and inferior endplates for disc attachment
  • Nutrient foramina for blood vessels
  • Attachment sites for ligaments

Vertebral Arch

The vertebral arch extends posteriorly from the body and, together with the posterior surface of the body, forms the vertebral foramen that houses and protects the spinal cord. The arch consists of two pedicles and two laminae.

  • Pedicles: Short, thick processes that project posteriorly from the body
  • Laminae: Broad, flat plates that extend from the pedicles to meet in the midline

The vertebral arch also contains articular facets that form joints with adjacent vertebrae.

Vertebral Processes

Each vertebra typically has seven processes that serve as attachment points for muscles and ligaments and form articulations with adjacent vertebrae:

  • Spinous Process: Projects posteriorly from the junction of the laminae
  • Transverse Processes (2): Project laterally from the junction of the pedicle and lamina
  • Superior Articular Processes (2): Project upward from the junction of the pedicle and lamina
  • Inferior Articular Processes (2): Project downward from the junction of the pedicle and lamina

The orientation and morphology of these processes vary by region of the spine.

Vertebral Foramina

Several openings or foramina are present in vertebrae:

  • Vertebral Foramen: The large central opening formed by the body and arch that contains the spinal cord
  • Intervertebral Foramina: Formed between adjacent vertebrae, allowing passage of spinal nerves
  • Transverse Foramina: Present only in cervical vertebrae, transmit the vertebral arteries, veins, and sympathetic nerves
  • Nutrient Foramina: Small openings in the body for blood vessels

These openings are crucial for the passage of neurovascular structures throughout the spine.

Research Applications

Our whole spine collection supports various research applications:

Biomechanical Analysis

Study the structural properties and mechanical behavior of the vertebral column through digital modeling.

Pathological Assessment

Examine vertebral pathologies and their effects on spinal function and morphology.

Comparative Anatomy

Compare vertebral morphology across different individuals, populations, or species.

Clinical Applications

Support development of surgical techniques, implant design, and treatment approaches for spinal conditions.
